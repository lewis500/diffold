As long as nineteenth-centry spellbinders of science could point to the planets and the Milky Way,...they held their audience. But with nothing to see except a blackboard strewn with differential equations, the educated man with an informed interest in science melted away.
Jacques Barzun, The Use and Abuse of Art (1974)

The phrase "differential equation" is an intimidating pair of science words, like "flux capacitor" or "tachyon field." If what Barzun says is true, just looking at them melted someone. But fear not. The goal here is to make them more friendly, to show off one of the most wonderful tricks we have to interpret and redesign the world.

Definition

You're probably used to regular old equations like $ t^2 +t = 2 $. Here, "solving the equation" means finding numbers that make the statement true. Below, change $ t $ until it's true that $ t^2 + t = 2 $.

A differential equation, by contrast, is a statement that involves the derivative of a function, and solving a differential equation means finding a function that makes the statement true. The answer is not a number; it's a factory that takes numbers and spits new ones out.

One Equation to Rule Them All

Suppose you have $1$ in debt. If that amount grows constantly at rate $k$, how much do you owe at time $t$? To answer this, we need to solve this differential equation:

$$ y' = k\cdot y. $$

This says, "How fast $y$ changes depends on $y$." When we solve it, we'll have the function $y(t)$ we want.

Now let's use differential equations to solve a problem. Suppose that you launch a roller skate in some direction with velocty $v_0$. As it rolls, friction slows it down according to the relationship

$$ \dot v(t) = -kv(t) $$

that is, the rate its velocity changes is negatively related to the velocity. So the faster something moves, the faster it slows down.

The solution to this equation is $v(t) = v_0 \cdot e^{-kt}.$
